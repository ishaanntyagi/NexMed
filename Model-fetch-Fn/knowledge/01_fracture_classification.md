# Fracture Classification Basics

## By pattern

- **Transverse**: fracture line roughly perpendicular to the bone's long axis. Typically from direct force.
- **Oblique**: angled fracture line. Often from indirect, angled force.
- **Spiral**: twisting fracture line; suggests a rotational/torsional mechanism. In children, always consider non-accidental injury in unclear histories.
- **Comminuted**: three or more fragments. Usually high-energy trauma.
- **Greenstick**: incomplete fracture with one cortex broken and the other bent. Almost exclusively pediatric.
- **Segmental**: two separate fracture lines isolating a middle segment of bone.

## By skin integrity

- **Closed**: skin intact over the fracture site.
- **Open (compound)**: skin breached; bone may or may not be visible. All open fractures need urgent irrigation, IV antibiotics, and tetanus review. Gustilo-Anderson is the common grading system (Type I small clean wound, Type II larger contaminated, Type III extensive soft-tissue damage).

## By displacement

- **Non-displaced**: fragments maintain anatomical alignment.
- **Displaced**: fragments separated, angulated, rotated, or shortened.

## By location on the bone

- **Diaphyseal**: mid-shaft.
- **Metaphyseal**: between shaft and end (growth region in children).
- **Epiphyseal**: involving the joint end. In children, Salter-Harris classification is used for growth-plate injuries (Types I–V).

## Clinical note

Documenting pattern, skin status, displacement, and location in that order keeps reporting consistent and helps downstream decision-making around fixation choice.
