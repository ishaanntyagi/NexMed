# Surgical vs Conservative Management

## When conservative (cast/splint) management is usually sufficient

- Non-displaced or minimally displaced fractures
- Stable fracture patterns (e.g., non-displaced distal radius, simple clavicle midshaft in children)
- Fractures in anatomically favorable sites with good healing biology
- Patients unfit for surgery where functional outcome after closed treatment is acceptable

## Indications for surgical fixation

Most real cases involve multiple reasons from the categories below.

### Absolute or near-absolute indications

- **Open fractures** requiring debridement and stabilization
- **Neurovascular compromise** requiring exploration or repair
- **Displaced intra-articular fractures** — preserving joint congruity matters for long-term function
- **Irreducible dislocations or fracture-dislocations**
- **Pathological fractures** requiring stabilization for pain or function
- **Compartment syndrome** requiring fasciotomy alongside fixation

### Relative indications

- Displaced fractures that cannot be held reduced in a cast
- Unstable fracture patterns (e.g., displaced femoral neck, segmental tibia)
- Polytrauma — early fixation aids nursing, mobilization, and respiratory outcomes
- Bilateral injuries or floating joints where mobilization depends on fixation
- Non-union or malunion after prior conservative treatment
- Patient-specific factors: early return-to-function needs, inability to comply with casting

## Common fixation methods

- **K-wires**: small, percutaneous; hand/wrist, pediatric fractures
- **Plates and screws (ORIF)**: open reduction, rigid fixation; most intra-articular and diaphyseal fractures
- **Intramedullary nails**: load-sharing devices; long bones (femur, tibia, humerus)
- **External fixators**: when soft tissues are too damaged for internal fixation, or for damage-control orthopedics
- **Arthroplasty**: for elderly displaced femoral neck fractures, or unreconstructable joint fractures

## Decision framing

The choice is rarely just "surgery yes/no". It's a balance of: fracture stability, soft tissue status, patient physiology, functional demands, resources available, and surgeon experience. Document the reasoning, not just the decision.
