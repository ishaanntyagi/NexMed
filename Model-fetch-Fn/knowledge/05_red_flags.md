# Red Flags and Escalation Triggers

Red flags are findings that should change the tempo of care — from routine assessment to urgent intervention. Miss them and recoverable injuries become disabling.

## Limb-threatening red flags

- **Neurovascular compromise**: absent distal pulse, pallor, paresthesia, paralysis, pain disproportionate to injury, pain on passive stretch (the classic "6 Ps"). Suggests arterial injury or compartment syndrome.
- **Open fracture**: any skin breach over a fracture, however small. Time to debridement matters.
- **Compartment syndrome**: tense swelling, severe pain out of proportion, pain on passive stretch of the involved muscles. Measure compartment pressures if available. Fasciotomy within 6 hours of onset gives the best outcomes.
- **Suspected vascular injury**: expanding hematoma, bruit, or pulsatile bleeding. Common with knee dislocation, supracondylar humerus fractures in children.

## Life-threatening red flags

- **Pelvic fracture with hemodynamic instability**: major bleeding source; needs pelvic binder and urgent transfer.
- **Femoral shaft fracture**: can lose 1–1.5 L of blood into the thigh. Bilateral femur fractures can be life-threatening.
- **Multiple long-bone fractures**: high risk of fat embolism syndrome within 24–72 hours.
- **Cervical spine injury with neurology**: full spinal precautions, no movement without clearance.

## Non-accidental injury (NAI) red flags

Particularly in pediatric and elderly populations:

- Injury inconsistent with the reported mechanism
- Delayed presentation without explanation
- Multiple injuries at different stages of healing
- Spiral fractures of humerus or femur in non-ambulatory children
- History that changes between tellings
- Bruising in unusual locations or in non-mobile infants

If NAI is suspected, escalate per local safeguarding policy. Do not confront the caregiver alone.

## Patient-specific escalation triggers

- Elderly patient with hip fracture → urgent orthogeriatric pathway
- Known or suspected malignancy with pathological fracture → oncology referral alongside orthopedic fixation
- Immunocompromised patient with open fracture → extended antibiotic coverage, lower threshold for admission
- Anticoagulated patient with any fracture → reverse as appropriate before surgery, bleeding risk assessment

## Communication

When escalating, hand over using a structured format (e.g., SBAR — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation). Include neurovascular status, analgesia given, and time of last oral intake. Vague handovers cause delays.
