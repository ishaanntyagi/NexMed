# Fracture Healing Timelines

## The four biological stages

1. **Hematoma formation** (first few hours to days): bleeding at the fracture site forms a clot that becomes the scaffold for healing.
2. **Inflammatory phase** (days 1–7): cytokine release, cellular recruitment, removal of debris.
3. **Callus formation** (weeks 2–6): soft callus (cartilage) bridges the gap, then gradually ossifies into hard callus.
4. **Remodeling** (months to years): woven bone is replaced by lamellar bone along lines of mechanical stress.

## Typical time to clinical union (adults, uncomplicated)

These are rough averages — actual healing varies with age, site, displacement, and patient factors.

- **Phalanges (fingers/toes)**: 3–6 weeks
- **Metacarpals / metatarsals**: 4–8 weeks
- **Distal radius (wrist)**: 6–8 weeks
- **Humeral shaft**: 8–12 weeks
- **Clavicle**: 6–12 weeks
- **Tibial shaft**: 12–16 weeks (notoriously slow; watch for non-union)
- **Femoral shaft**: 12–16 weeks
- **Scaphoid**: 8–12 weeks for non-displaced; longer and risk of non-union for displaced
- **Pelvis**: 8–12 weeks depending on pattern and stability

## Pediatric healing

Children heal roughly twice as fast as adults for the same site, and have greater remodeling capacity — small angulations near the growth plate often correct themselves over time. Beware: growth-plate (Salter-Harris) injuries can cause growth arrest.

## Factors that delay healing

- **Patient**: smoking, diabetes, poor nutrition, advanced age, NSAID use (debated), corticosteroids
- **Injury**: open fractures, high-energy trauma, comminution, segmental loss, infection
- **Mechanical**: inadequate immobilization, early weight-bearing, poor reduction, gap at fracture site

## Delayed union vs non-union

- **Delayed union**: healing slower than expected but still progressing.
- **Non-union**: healing has stopped; fracture line persists beyond 6–9 months with no progressive callus. Often requires surgical intervention (bone grafting, revision fixation).
